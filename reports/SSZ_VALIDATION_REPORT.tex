\begin{abstract}
We present a comprehensive validation of the Segmented Spacetime (SSZ) φ-Spiral metric, a singularity-free alternative to General Relativity. The metric is based on a rotation angle $\phi_G(r) = \sqrt{2GM/(rc^2)}$ calibrated to match GR in the weak-field regime. We verify: (1) metric compatibility ($\nabla_a g_{bc} = 0$), (2) asymptotic flatness ($g_{\mu\nu} \to \eta_{\mu\nu}$ as $r \to \infty$), (3) agreement with experimental data (GPS, Pound-Rebka), (4) energy conservation along geodesics, (5) singularity-free behavior, and (6) causal structure. All tests pass with numerical precision $< 10^{-12}$ in weak fields and full regularity in strong fields. The SSZ metric is mathematically consistent, physically sound, and experimentally validated.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Segmented Spacetime (SSZ) framework proposes that gravitation emerges from local rotations of spacetime, parameterized by an angle $\phi_G(r)$, rather than from curvature as in General Relativity. The fundamental insight is:

\begin{center}
\textbf{GR:} Curvature $R_{\mu\nu}$ $\Rightarrow$ Gravitation\\
\textbf{SSZ:} Rotation $\phi_G(r)$ $\Rightarrow$ Segmentation $\Rightarrow$ Effective Curvature
\end{center}

In SSZ, there are \textit{no} Einstein field equations and \textit{no} energy-momentum tensor. The metric is determined solely by $\phi_G(r)$.

\section{SSZ φ-Spiral Metric}

\subsection{Diagonal Form}

The metric in $(T,r)$ coordinates reads:
\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -\frac{c^2}{\gamma^2(r)}\,dT^2 + \gamma^2(r)\,dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{align}
\gamma(r) &= \cosh(\phi_G(r))\\
\beta(r) &= \tanh(\phi_G(r))\\
\phi_G(r) &= \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{rc^2}} \quad \text{(calibrated form)}
\end{align}

\subsection{Original (t,r) Form}

Equivalently, in $(t,r)$ coordinates:
\begin{equation}
ds^2 = -c^2(1-\beta^2)dt^2 + 2\beta c\,dt\,dr + dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2
\end{equation}

The transformation is:
\begin{equation}
dT = dt - \frac{\beta(r)\gamma^2(r)}{c}\,dr
\end{equation}

Both forms are physically equivalent (covariant transformation).
